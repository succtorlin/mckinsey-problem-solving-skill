# McKinsey 7-Step Problem-Solving Process
## Detailed Reference

---

## Overview

The 7-step process is McKinsey's canonical structured approach to solving any complex problem. It is iterative, not strictly linear — teams move back and forth between steps as new information emerges.

```
1. Define the problem
2. Structure the problem
3. Prioritize the issues
4. Plan analyses and work
5. Conduct analyses and work
6. Synthesize findings
7. Communicate recommendations
```

---

## Step 1: Define the Problem

**Goal:** Produce a precise, agreed-upon problem statement before any analysis begins.

**Key activities:**
- Align all stakeholders on what problem is actually being solved.
- Clarify the decision context: Who is the decision maker? What are their constraints? What is the timeline?
- Identify what is in scope and out of scope.
- Debate the exact wording — every word matters ("or" vs. "and", "should we" vs. "how do we").

**Common failure mode:** Teams jump to analysis before the problem is clearly defined, then discover they solved the wrong problem.

**Deliverable:** A one-sentence problem statement using the template: "[Action verb] [what] for [whom] by [when] given [constraints], in order to [desired outcome]."

**Time investment:** McKinsey teams often spend 20–30% of total project time on problem definition. It is the highest-leverage step.

---

## Step 2: Structure the Problem

**Goal:** Disaggregate the problem into a MECE issue tree that maps all possible causes, drivers, or solutions.

**Key activities:**
- Build 2–3 different issue trees using different structuring approaches (e.g., by value chain, by customer segment, by financial driver).
- Each tree gives a different perspective on where the answer might be.
- Do not yet prioritize — the goal is to ensure completeness.

**Structuring approaches:**
- **Process-based:** Break the problem along the steps of a process (e.g., value chain, customer journey).
- **Component-based:** Break the problem into its mathematical or logical components (e.g., Profit = Revenue − Cost).
- **Hypothesis-based:** List all plausible hypotheses and group them into MECE categories.

**Deliverable:** A visual issue tree (on paper or whiteboard) with 3–4 levels of branches, all MECE.

---

## Step 3: Prioritize the Issues

**Goal:** Identify the 2–3 branches of the issue tree most likely to contain the answer and most worth investigating.

**Key activities:**
- Apply the prioritization matrix: impact × movability.
- Use initial data, intuition, and stakeholder input to form early hypotheses about which branches matter most.
- Explicitly deprioritize branches that are small, immovable, or already well-understood.

**Key question:** "If we could only investigate one branch of this tree, which one would most likely change our recommendation?"

**Deliverable:** A ranked list of the top 2–3 priority issues with a brief rationale for each.

---

## Step 4: Plan the Work

**Goal:** Design the specific analyses needed to prove or disprove the priority hypotheses, and assign them to team members.

**Key activities:**
- Build a ghost deck: outline the story and the slides needed before any analysis is done.
- For each priority issue, define: What analysis is needed? What data is required? Who will do it? By when?
- Apply the 80/20 rule: identify the minimum analysis needed to reach a defensible conclusion.
- Set a "level-one answer" deadline — a rough answer based on available data — before committing to deeper analysis.

**Deliverable:** A work plan with assigned analyses, data sources, and timelines. A ghost deck with slide titles and analysis notes.

---

## Step 5: Conduct the Analyses

**Goal:** Execute the planned analyses efficiently, updating hypotheses as new information emerges.

**Key activities:**
- Collect data from primary sources (interviews, surveys) and secondary sources (databases, reports).
- Conduct quantitative analyses (financial modeling, statistical analysis, benchmarking).
- Conduct qualitative analyses (expert interviews, customer research, competitive intelligence).
- Continuously test the hypothesis: does the data support or refute it?
- Update the issue tree and work plan as new information changes priorities.

**Key discipline:** Avoid "analysis paralysis." Stop when you have enough evidence to make a defensible recommendation, not when you have perfect information.

**Common failure mode:** Teams collect all possible data before forming a view. McKinsey trains consultants to form a view early and update it with evidence.

---

## Step 6: Synthesize Findings

**Goal:** Convert analysis results into insight. Move from "what the data shows" to "what it means for the decision."

**Key activities:**
- Apply the "So What?" test to every piece of analysis: What is the key takeaway? What are the root causes? What is the implication?
- Identify the 3–5 key insights that together answer the problem statement.
- Check that insights are MECE: no overlap, no gaps.
- Build the pyramid: governing thought → key line arguments → supporting evidence.

**Synthesis vs. summary:**
- Summary: "Revenue declined 15%."
- Synthesis: "Revenue declined 15% due to pricing pressure from a new competitor, not volume loss — meaning the fix is pricing strategy, not sales capacity."

**Deliverable:** A set of 3–5 "so what" statements that collectively answer the problem statement.

---

## Step 7: Communicate Recommendations

**Goal:** Present findings and recommendations in a clear, structured, and persuasive way that drives action.

**Key activities:**
- Structure the presentation using the Pyramid Principle: lead with the answer, support with key arguments, back with evidence.
- Open with the SCQA framework: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer.
- Write every slide title as a complete sentence conveying the insight (not a topic label).
- Anticipate objections and address them proactively.
- End with clear, specific, prioritized next steps.

**Key principle:** Clients do not pay for analysis — they pay for recommendations. Every communication should end with "therefore, we recommend..."

**Deliverable:** A polished presentation or memo structured as: Executive Summary → Key Findings → Recommendations → Implementation Plan → Appendix.

---

## The Iterative Nature of the Process

Steps 2–6 are often repeated as new information emerges:

```
Define → Structure → Prioritize → Plan → Analyze → [New info] → Re-prioritize → Re-plan → Re-analyze → Synthesize → Communicate
```

The governing thought in the ghost deck is updated as analysis progresses. This is called "iterating on the hypothesis."

**Key principle:** The process is a scaffold for creativity, not a rigid checklist. The goal is always to find the best answer, not to follow the steps perfectly.
