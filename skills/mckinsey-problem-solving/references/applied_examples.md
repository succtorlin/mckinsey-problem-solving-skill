# McKinsey Problem-Solving: Applied Examples
## How to apply the methodology to real tasks

---

## Example 1: Business Problem Analysis

**Trigger:** "Our profits have declined. What should we do?"

**Step 1 — Problem statement:**
"Determine the primary cause of the 20% profit decline in the North American division over the past 12 months, and identify the 2–3 highest-impact actions to recover profitability within 18 months."

**Step 2 — Issue tree (diagnostic):**
```
Profit decline
├── Revenue decline
│   ├── Price decline
│   │   ├── Pricing pressure from competitors
│   │   └── Product mix shift to lower-margin items
│   └── Volume decline
│       ├── Market share loss
│       └── Market size contraction
└── Cost increase
    ├── Fixed cost increase
    │   ├── Headcount / overhead growth
    │   └── Facility / infrastructure costs
    └── Variable cost increase
        ├── Input cost inflation
        └── Operational inefficiency
```

**Step 3 — Prioritize:**
Initial hypothesis: "Revenue decline is the primary driver, specifically market share loss due to a new competitor." Prioritize: (1) market share data, (2) pricing analysis, (3) competitor intelligence.

**Step 4 — Ghost deck headlines:**
1. "Profits declined 20% primarily due to a 15% revenue drop, not cost increases."
2. "Revenue loss was driven by market share loss in the mid-market segment, not pricing."
3. "A new competitor captured 8 points of market share by undercutting on price in Q2."
4. "Three actions can recover profitability: targeted repricing, product bundling, and mid-market sales investment."

**Step 6 — Synthesis (So What):**
"The profit decline is a competitive response problem, not an operational one. The fix is a targeted pricing and sales strategy in the mid-market segment, not cost cutting."

## Example 2: Strategy Question

**Trigger:** "Should we enter the Japanese market?"

**Step 1 — Problem statement:**
"Determine whether to enter the Japanese market with our premium product line within 18 months, given a $50M investment cap, in order to achieve 5% market share by Year 3."

**Step 2 — Issue tree (decision):**
```
Should we enter Japan?
├── Is the market attractive?
│   ├── Market size and growth rate
│   ├── Competitive intensity
│   └── Regulatory environment
├── Can we win?
│   ├── Product-market fit
│   ├── Competitive advantage in Japan
│   └── Route to market (distribution, partnerships)
└── Is it worth it financially?
    ├── Expected revenue and margin
    ├── Investment required
    └── Risk-adjusted return vs. alternatives
```

**Step 3 — Prioritize:**
Hypothesis: "The market is attractive but we lack a viable route to market." Prioritize: (1) distribution channel analysis, (2) competitive landscape, (3) financial modeling.

**Pyramid structure for recommendation:**
- **Governing thought:** "We recommend entering Japan through a joint venture with [Partner X], targeting the premium B2B segment, with a phased $30M investment over 24 months."
- **Key argument 1:** Japan's premium B2B market is large ($2B) and growing at 8% annually with limited direct competition.
- **Key argument 2:** A joint venture with [Partner X] provides immediate distribution access and regulatory expertise, reducing entry risk by 60%.
- **Key argument 3:** Financial modeling shows a 3-year payback and 25% IRR under conservative assumptions.

## Example 3: Applying SCQA to a Memo or Presentation Opening

**Context:** Presenting findings on customer churn to the CEO.

**Bad opening (data dump):**
"We analyzed 12 months of customer data across 5 segments. We found that churn rates varied from 8% to 22% across segments. The highest churn was in the SMB segment. We also found that NPS scores correlated with churn at r=0.72..."

**Good opening (SCQA):**
- **Situation:** "Our customer base has grown 30% over the past two years, and retention has historically been our key competitive advantage."
- **Complication:** "However, churn in the SMB segment has increased from 8% to 22% over the past 12 months, putting $4M in annual recurring revenue at risk."
- **Question:** "What is driving the SMB churn spike, and what should we do about it?"
- **Answer:** "Our analysis shows that 80% of SMB churn is driven by onboarding failure in the first 90 days. We recommend a dedicated SMB onboarding program that we project will reduce churn to 10% and protect $3.2M in ARR."

## Example 4: Structuring a Response in a Meeting

**Context:** A senior executive asks: "What do you think about our pricing strategy?"

**Unstructured response:** "Well, there are a lot of factors to consider. Pricing depends on the market, the competition, our costs, customer willingness to pay, and our positioning. I think we should look at all of these before making a decision..."

**McKinsey-structured response (Pyramid Principle):**
"My view is that our current pricing is leaving money on the table in the enterprise segment, and we should test a 15% price increase there. [Governing thought first.] Three things support this: First, our enterprise customers have a 40% lower churn rate than SMB, suggesting higher perceived value. Second, our price is 20% below the nearest competitor in that segment. Third, our gross margin in enterprise is already 78%, so there's room to invest the incremental revenue in retention programs. I'd recommend a 60-day A/B test before a full rollout."

## Example 5: Applying the "So What?" Test to Slide Titles

**Context:** Presenting market research findings.

| Bad Title (Topic) | Good Title (Insight with So What) |
|---|---|
| "Market Size Analysis" | "The $48B market is growing at 12% CAGR, making it venture-backable" |
| "Competitive Landscape" | "AristAI is the only full-suite AI-native platform — all competitors are point solutions" |
| "Customer Survey Results" | "90% of surveyed CIOs cite compliance cost as their #1 pain point, validating our entry strategy" |
| "Financial Projections" | "Conservative projections show $140M ARR by 2028 with a 45-day sales cycle assumption" |
| "Team Background" | "Our team's unique combination of AI depth and accreditor relationships is a defensible moat" |

## Quick Diagnostic: Is My Thinking McKinsey-Quality?

Use this checklist before presenting any analysis or recommendation:

| Check | Question |
|---|---|
| Problem definition | Can I state the problem in one precise sentence with an action verb? |
| MECE | Is my breakdown free of overlaps and gaps? |
| Hypothesis-driven | Did I form a hypothesis before analyzing, or did I analyze everything? |
| 80/20 | Am I doing the minimum analysis needed to reach a defensible conclusion? |
| So What | Can I state the implication of every finding for the decision maker? |
| Pyramid | Am I leading with the answer, or burying it at the end? |
| SCQA | Does my opening create tension and make the audience want to hear the answer? |
| Ghost deck | Did I outline the story before building the slides? |
